Story Pool — 一个故事覆盖多道题

最少准备 6-7 个核心故事 即可覆盖所有 BQ 题。

故事 A：难相处同事 / 跨团队协作冲突

  • Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a colleague. How did you handle it?
  • Tell me about a time a colleague was not cooperative. How did you handle it?
  • Tell me about a time you worked with a difficult coworker. How did you handle the situation?
  • Describe a time you had to collaborate with another team that was working on something similar.

核心要素：冲突起因 → 你主动沟通/找对齐 → 达成合作 → 结果

故事 B：向上管理 / 对高级别的异见

  • Tell me about a time you disagreed with a senior colleague or stakeholder and how you handled it.
  • Describe a time there was a dispute over credit or recognition and how you handled it.

核心要素：立场分歧 → 数据/逻辑支撑你的观点 → 如何 respectfully push back → 结果

故事 C：帮助低绩效同事 / 主动帮助他人

  • Tell me about a time you helped an underperforming peer. What did you do?
  • Tell me about a time you went out of your way to help someone outside your team.

核心要素：识别对方的卡点 → 主动介入 → 具体帮助方式 → 对方/团队的改变

故事 D：任务分配被 pushback（Leadership）

  • Tell me about a time you had to assign tasks to a team and they pushed back on your assignments.

故事 E：旗舰项目 / 最有成就感 ⭐

  • Describe the most challenging project you have worked on.
  • Tell me about your greatest professional achievement or most successful project.
  • Tell me about a time you went above and beyond expectations.
  • How would you plan and deliver a new feature from scratch?

最重要的故事，建议准备最充分、细节最丰富，能拆成多个角度讲。

故事 F：模糊 / 紧急 / 多任务压力下的交付

  • Tell me about a time you had to deliver under a very tight deadline.
  • How do you handle constantly changing priorities? Describe a specific example.
  • Describe a time you had to handle an ambiguous situation with no clear solution.
  • Tell me about a time you had to manage multiple projects at the same time.
  • Describe a situation where there was no clear solution and you had to figure out what to do.
  • Tell me about a time a project changed direction midway. How did you adapt?

高度重叠，一个「项目中途变向 + deadline 压缩」的故事可以全部搞定。

故事 G：失败 / 犯错 / 弱点

  • Describe a time you failed to meet a goal. What happened and what did you learn?
  • Tell me about a mistake you made at work and how you handled it.
  • What is your biggest weakness and how are you improving it?

核心要素：坦诚的 failure → 你如何复盘 → 具体改进行动 → 后续结果

故事 H：学习新东西并应用

  • Tell me about a time you learned something new and applied it to your work.

开放性问题（需单独准备，不套故事）

  • What would you want to work on at Google and why?
  • What keeps you motivated and passionate about your work?
  • What is your greatest achievement outside of work?
  • How will you keep contributing and stay relevant at Google?

Story 1: Content Moderation Service Failure → Client Saved

Situation

Our team owns a content moderation service built on Amazon Bedrock, responsible for filtering spam and profanity in comments and articles. One of our key clients — the AWA Builder Center team — experienced a sudden spam attack where airline ads flooded their platform. Our service failed to catch them, resulting in over 500 support tickets being created on the client side in a short window. The client was seriously considering migrating to a different moderation provider because of this incident.

Task

I took ownership of diagnosing and upgrading our moderation system to prevent this from happening again — and more immediately, to rebuild the client's confidence before they made the decision to leave.

Action

Phase 1 — Evaluation Framework: I built an evaluation framework using the dataset the client provided from the attack. I systematically benchmarked different models and parameters — including temperature, top-p, and top-k — measuring F1 score, false positive rate, false negative rate, latency, and cost. This gave us a data-driven basis for choosing the best model configuration.

Phase 2 — Prompt Optimization: After identifying the optimal combination and upgrading the model, spam detection improved significantly. But I noticed we were over-flagging legitimate content. I redesigned the system prompt following prompt engineering best practices: adding few-shot examples with explicit input-output pairs, defining clear roles and rules, and structuring the prompt in XML format to give the model cleaner context boundaries.

Result

MetricBeforeAfterChange
Accuracy68.2%93.2%+25 pts
False Positives44899-78%
False Negatives675140-79%
Weekly Tickets~500~10-20-97%

The client decided to stay. The evaluation framework is now the standard process for any model upgrade.

Follow-up Preparation

What did you learn? Before this, we had no systematic evaluation framework — we tuned parameters on intuition. Now, every model upgrade must go through a standardized benchmark process.

What would you do differently? I would have established the evaluation framework proactively rather than reactively. Having benchmark data ready before incidents happen gives you confidence when things go wrong.

Story 2: Pipeline Architecture Disagreement with Senior Engineer

Situation

I was on the AWS Marketing Content Validation team, collaborating with the Event Content Orchestration team to build an end-to-end automated workflow — publishing AWS event metadata from Airtable to multiple end platforms. The goal was to eliminate manual Excel uploads and edits entirely.

At the time, the mobile channel was already live in production, and we needed to extend the system to support two additional channels: web and a third-party platform.

The disagreement came early: a senior engineer strongly advocated for keeping all three channels in a single shared pipeline, arguing it would reduce operational overhead long-term. My team's position was that parallel development across three channels made a decoupled, multi-pipeline architecture the better choice.

Task

My job was to move this from an opinions-based debate to a data-driven architectural decision, and to get senior stakeholder buy-in on the decoupled approach.

Action

Rather than continuing an informal back-and-forth, I wrote a formal design doc comparing the two architectures across five dimensions:

  1. Parallel development velocity — with a shared pipeline, progress on web or 3P could block mobile's promotion to production
  2. Blast radius — making rapid changes to a prod pipeline that mobile already depends on carries real risk
  3. Rollback granularity — a single pipeline means any rollback affects all channels simultaneously
  4. Dependency isolation — decoupling prevents one channel's instability from cascading into others
  5. Operational load — the doc quantified why the other four factors outweighed maintaining multiple configs

Result

The senior engineer aligned with the decoupled approach. We moved forward with separate pipelines per channel, unblocking parallel development and giving each team independent rollback control. The formal doc became a reference for future architecture decisions in the team.

Follow-up Preparation

Was the senior engineer genuinely convinced? He raised valid concerns about operational overhead, which I acknowledged in the doc. We agreed to revisit if the maintenance cost proved higher than expected. Six months later, the team agreed the decoupled approach was the right call.

What did you learn? When you disagree with a senior engineer, don't make it personal. Bring data, write it down, and frame it as a collaborative decision rather than a debate to win.

Story 3: Event Content Publishing Automation — Tight Deadline

Situation

I was an engineer on the Adabase Marketing Content Validation team. Our team owns a content moderation service that validates all content before publication — catching internal terms, broken links, and violation content.

We were brought into a cross-team initiative to automate AWS Event content publishing across three teams:

TeamRole
Our Team (Content Validator & Content Event Workflow)Content validation + event workflow Step Function
AI Orchestrator TeamRoutes content to different channels, invokes workflow Step Functions
Client Team (e.g. Event Mobile)Consumes validated metadata into S3, renders in mobile app

The existing process was entirely manual: marketers downloaded Excel files from Airtable, manually validated every row, uploaded to Jenkins, and published to S3. We had only two weeks to design and build the automation from scratch.

Task

My specific responsibility was the Mobile channel: designing a serverless pipeline to (1) pull metadata from Airtable and transform, (2) run through Content Validation, (3) push to the Client Team's S3 bucket — all while coordinating across three teams.

Action

Cross-Team Coordination: I mapped out every integration point. The AI Orchestrator Team would invoke our workflow Step Function, and we would deliver to the Client Team's S3. I set up daily 15-minute standups across teams.

Ambiguity — S3 Schema Alignment: The Client Team's mobile app consumed metadata in a specific nested JSON structure, but there was no documentation. I reverse-engineered the expected format from existing S3 data, built a mapping document, and walked through it with the Client Team's lead engineer. We caught mismatches like session speakers needing a nested array vs. our flat speaker_name string.

Architecture Challenge — Agent Concurrency Bottleneck:

Original architecture:

Step Function
  └─ Lambda (per content item)
       └─ Content Generation Agent
            └─ MCP call → Content Validator

The agent had a service quota limiting concurrent invocations to ~10. For hundreds of session entries, this was a massive bottleneck.

Optimized architecture:

Step Function
  └─ Lambda (collects ALL content items)
       └─ Single Content Generation Agent
            └─ Skill → Batch Content Validator Lambda
                 └─ Validates ALL items in one invocation

This bypassed the concurrency limit — from 10 items at a time to an entire event's session catalog in one shot.

Week 1: Built Step Functions + 3 Lambdas (Fetch & Transform, Batch Validate, Publish).

Week 2: End-to-end integration, retry logic + exponential backoff, human-readable SNS notifications.

Result

  • 94% reduction in manual effort — saving 4,300+ hours annually across 36 Flagship Events
  • Content owners publish in minutes instead of days, directly from Airtable
  • Delivered in 4 weeks end-to-end (workflow built in 2 weeks)
  • Post-launch bug found and fixed same day
  • Now live for Sydney and Paris AWS events; Phases 1.1–3 underway
  • Acknowledged by name in the launch announcement

Follow-up Preparation

Biggest technical challenge? The agent concurrency bottleneck. The ~10 invocation limit meant the original architecture would have failed at scale. Redesigning to batch model required rethinking the entire validation flow.

How did you handle the S3 schema ambiguity? I didn't wait for documentation. I reverse-engineered from production data, created a mapping document, and drove a review session. When requirements are unclear, produce a concrete artifact people can react to.

How did you coordinate across three teams on a two-week deadline? Daily 15-minute standups, a clear integration map, and immediate escalation when any dependency was blocked.

Story 4: Helping Another Team Debug Flaky Integration Tests

Situation

This happened during the Event Content Publishing Automation project — the same cross-team initiative. An engineer on the AI Orchestrator Team was responsible for the First Party Event workflow. He had been stuck on a frustrating issue: his integration tests would fail intermittently with no clear root cause — sometimes passing, then failing with no code changes. This was blocking his progress on the two-week deadline.

I was not assigned to help him. I noticed his message in our shared Slack channel and recognized the symptoms immediately.

Task

I had dealt with a similar issue on my own workflow: when the Content Generation Agent called the Content Validator MCP, something would occasionally go wrong, resulting in a 400 Error (input invalid). I had my own Mobile channel to deliver on deadline — this was not my scope. But I knew how demoralizing flaky tests can be. I decided to step in.

Action

Step 1 — Form a hypothesis: I had previously encountered a bug where the Content Generation Agent would modify the input using its own knowledge rather than passing through the original input as-is. The agent might change enabled_modules, locale, or content_source — "helpful corrections" that were actually wrong. The Content Validator Lambda would reject these with a 400 Error. Because these modifications were non-deterministic, test failures were intermittent — exactly matching his symptoms. I messaged him directly with my hypothesis.

Step 2 — Prove with data: I went into CloudWatch Logs, located his workflow's log group, and ran a targeted query filtering for 400 errors. The logs confirmed it — each failed test was preceded by a 400 Error with incorrect values in the modified fields. I screenshotted the evidence, annotated the causal chain, and shared it.

Step 3 — Fix: I drafted updated prompt rules for the skill: agent must pass through original input unchanged, must not use its own knowledge to modify any field values. I walked him through the changes in a 15-minute call. Tests passed consistently for the first time in days.

Result

  • First Party Event workflow integration tests fully stabilized
  • Colleague unblocked and delivered on the two-week timeline
  • The updated prompt rules became the standard pattern for all agent-to-MCP interactions
  • Validated my earlier fix on Mobile channel — proved it was a systemic issue
  • Project shipped on time across all channels

Follow-up Preparation

Why did you help when you had your own deadline? Two reasons: (1) I recognized the symptoms and knew this bug could waste days — sharing context could save him hours. (2) If it was systemic, it would hit my workflow too — fixing it proactively was in everyone's interest.

What made this agent behavior hard to catch? The agent's input modification was non-deterministic — it didn't always happen, and it didn't always change the same fields. This looked like a flaky network issue, which is why my colleague was stuck. Only targeted log analysis revealed the pattern.

What systemic improvement came from this? We added the "pass-through input" rule to the standard prompt template for all agent-MCP interactions. I also documented the CloudWatch log query pattern so others could quickly diagnose similar issues.
